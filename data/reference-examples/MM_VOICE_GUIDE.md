# Docebo Mid-Market Brand Voice & Messaging Guide

A companion to the Docebo Brand Voice Guide for campaigns targeting mid-market (1,000–5,000 employee) organizations.

**Status:** V1 — For Review
**Date:** May 2026

---

## 1. Why This Document Exists

Docebo's existing Brand Voice Guide ("The Learning Insurgent") and Phoenix Brand Guide define the voice that has driven enterprise engagement to a 6% account engagement rate on LinkedIn. That voice is working.

It is not working for mid-market. Accounts between 1,000 and 5,000 employees engage at roughly 0.5% — one-twelfth the enterprise rate — on the same ads, same spend, same creative window. The problem is not budget or targeting. The problem is message-market fit.

This document does not replace the existing brand voice guides. It extends them with a mid-market variation layer: adjusted tone dials, reframed pillars, a new persona, and copy rules specifically for campaigns targeting mid-market buyers. Every principle in the original guides still applies unless this document explicitly overrides it for the MM context.

> Use this guide any time creative is tagged "MM" in the Docebo Creative Monitor. For enterprise-tagged creative, defer to the original Brand Voice Guide and Phoenix Brand Guide.

---

## 2. The Diagnosis: Same Voice, Wrong Frequency

An audit of our top-performing LinkedIn ads and the 60-second CTV manifesto reveals a consistent pattern. Every piece of creative uses what we call the "Org-AI Frame":

- Aperture is company-wide AI strategy
- Proof is industry-level statistics (88%, 91%, 8-in-10)
- Visuals are glass towers, robot arms, sunset cities
- Implied buyer is a CLO, CHRO, or CRO with an org-wide AI mandate

That frame is precisely right for an enterprise buyer who builds board decks and needs vendor credibility. It is precisely wrong for a mid-market VP of L&D who owns one rollout, one team, and one quarter.

> The messaging is doing its job — for the wrong buyer.

### 2.1 The Core Insight

Mid-market is not a smaller enterprise. It is a different buyer with a different job, on a different timeline, asking a different question.

| Enterprise Buyer Asks: | Mid-Market Buyer Asks: |
|---|---|
| "How do I make my entire workforce AI-ready in 18 months?" | "Can I roll this out by Q3 and prove it worked?" |
| Time horizon: 12–24 months | Time horizon: This quarter |
| Deal shape: Committee, RFP, multi-region | Deal shape: VP with budget, lean buying group |
| Budget owner: CLO / CHRO / CRO | Budget owner: VP/Director of L&D |

Same product. Different story to tell about it.

## 3. The Mid-Market Persona: Maya Chen

- **Title:** VP of Learning & Development
- **Company:** 2,800-person SaaS company
- **Age:** 44, reports to CHRO
- **Team:** Leads a team of 6
- **Scope:** US-based, global program responsibility

### 3.1 Maya's Typical Tuesday

| Time | Activity |
|---|---|
| 8:30 AM | Slacks her LMS admin: "Why did the Workday sync fail again?" |
| 10:00 AM | Steerco update — has 7 minutes to defend last quarter's training spend |
| 12:30 PM | Lunch at desk, drafts CEO talking points for AI town hall |
| 2:00 PM | Kickoff with Sales Enablement on Q3 product launch certification |
| 4:30 PM | Searches LinkedIn for "how other VPs measured L&D ROI" |
| 6:15 PM | Closes laptop wondering if she's a strategist or a project manager |

### 3.2 What Maya Wants vs. What She's Running From

| More Of | Less Of |
|---|---|
| Visible wins her boss can quote in the next earnings call | "Why did completions drop?" emails on Sunday night |
| Hours back from chasing completion reports | Being the person who explains why training didn't move the needle |
| Credibility with finance, security, and IT in one breath | Vendor pitches built for someone twice her org's size |

### 3.3 Who Matters to Maya

- Her CHRO (her champion)
- The CFO (skeptical of L&D ROI)
- Her 6-person team (fiercely loyal, burned out)
- The CEO (drops a new initiative every Monday)

### 3.4 Maya's Timeline

- **Past:** Inherited a clunky LMS that everyone loved to hate.
- **Present:** Drowning in admin, asked to "do AI."
- **Future:** Wants to be the VP who made L&D matter — or get out before someone else has to.

## 4. Tribal Language: What Lands, What Repels

How Maya talks versus how a CLO talks. Most of our current copy uses the right column. Every mid-market ad must use the left.

| Lands with Maya ✅ | Repels Her (sounds like a vendor) ❌ |
|---|---|
| "completion rate" | "AI-powered learning ecosystem" |
| "my team" / "my admin" | "capability building at scale" |
| "Workday integration" | "once-in-a-generation shift" |
| "prove it worked" | "operating system for learning" |
| "in time for Q3" | "workforce of the future" |
| "my CHRO is asking" | "unlock your people's potential" |
| "our compliance audit" | "AI readiness gap" |
| "the Sales Enablement team needs…" | "transformative skills strategy" |

> **The litmus test:** Read every headline against the right column. If it could have come from the right column, rewrite it.

## 5. Mid-Market Voice Variation Rules

The three voice pillars from the Brand Voice Guide — Rebelliously Smart, Confidently Irreverent, and Addictively Human — still apply. But for mid-market, the dial settings change.

### 5.1 Tone Dial Adjustments

| Tone Dimension | Enterprise Setting | Mid-Market Setting |
|---|---|---|
| Swagger | 70% confident swagger | 50% empathetic confidence |
| Provocation | 20% playful provocation | 30% knowing solidarity |
| Technical precision | 10% technical precision | 20% tactical specificity |
| Status quo indignation | 30% righteous indignation | 40% "I've been there" validation |
| Scale of ambition | Org-wide transformation | This quarter, this team, this metric |
| Proof style | Industry statistics | Peer company before/after stories |
| AI framing | AI as strategic vision | AI as time-back-in-your-day leverage |

### 5.2 The Core Shift in One Line

> Enterprise voice says: *"We're the future of learning."*
> Mid-market voice says: ***"We've been in your shoes. Here's what actually worked."***

### 5.3 Voice Pillar Rewrites for MM

**Pillar 1: Rebelliously Smart → Practically Brilliant**

- Enterprise version: We drop knowledge bombs, not jargon bombs.
- MM version: We skip the buzzwords and show you the dashboard. If our insight doesn't save you a meeting this week, we're doing it wrong.
- ENT example: "Your people are brilliant. Your training shouldn't insult their intelligence."
- MM rewrite: "Your team of 6 just got asked to train 2,800 people. You don't need a pep talk. You need a platform that does the heavy lifting."

**Pillar 2: Confidently Irreverent → Knowing Solidarity**

- Enterprise version: We call BS on outdated training methods.
- MM version: We've seen every broken LMS rollout. We're not here to judge. We're here because your Sunday nights deserve better.
- ENT example: "Remember death by PowerPoint? We killed it."
- MM rewrite: "Your LMS is where learning goes to die. We both know it. Let's fix it before your next steerco."

**Pillar 3: Addictively Human → Effortlessly Lean**

- Enterprise version: Think Netflix met your learning platform.
- MM version: Think "what an enterprise team of 30 builds — your team of 6 ships." AI isn't a strategy slide. It's the reason your admin isn't rebuilding reports until 8 PM.
- ENT example: "So smooth, your teams will forget they're learning."
- MM rewrite: "6 people. 2,800 learners. Zero burnout. That's not a tagline. That's a Tuesday with Docebo."

## 6. Mid-Market Positioning Hierarchy

One core promise, three pillars, proof points Maya can screenshot.

> **CORE PROMISE: Make L&D the team your CFO finally takes seriously.**

### Pillar 1: Learning People Actually Finish

Completion is the floor, not the goal. Engagement is the proof.

- **Why it lands:** Maya inherited an LMS where 31% of learners ever finish a course. She's measured on it. So is her boss. Every other vendor talks about content libraries; nobody talks about whether anyone watches.
- **Proof points:** Named MM customer before/after completion rate; time-to-launch (weeks, not quarters); learner-side screenshot of the actual experience.
- **Voice example:** "If your training needs forced attendance, your training sucks." — This line is in the brand voice guide. It is currently nowhere in the ads. It should be everywhere in MM creative.

### Pillar 2: Your Team Is Too Small for Manual

AI as leverage for lean L&D shops — not as a future-of-work essay.

- **Why it lands:** Enterprise buys AI to look modern to the board. Maya buys AI to give her 6-person team back its weekends. The current ads frame AI as strategy. For MM, AI must be framed as leverage.
- **Proof points:** Specific automation ("X hours/week back" from a real customer); AI-generated content/translation use case (not the abstract version); headcount comparison ("What an enterprise team of 30 builds — your team of 6 ships").
- **Voice example:** "Your L&D team called. They want their lives back."

### Pillar 3: Prove L&D's Worth in One Screenshot

Outcomes Maya can paste into a Slack thread and end the argument.

- **Why it lands:** Maya's nightmare is the steerco where she can't connect L&D spend to a business number. Industry stats don't help her. A specific dashboard from a peer company does.
- **Proof points:** A real dashboard view (with permission) — not a stock illustration; outcome metric tied to revenue/retention, not learning hours; quote from the customer's CHRO or CFO, not their CLO.
- **Voice example:** "Acme went from 12% completion to 94% in 6 months. Their CFO stopped asking what L&D was for."

## 7. Copy Rules for Mid-Market Creative

### 7.1 Mandatory Substitutions

When writing MM copy, apply these replacements automatically:

| If the ENT Copy Says… | MM Copy Should Say… |
|---|---|
| "AI-powered learning ecosystem" | "the platform that handles [specific task]" |
| "Workforce of the future" | "Your team, this quarter" |
| "Organization-wide AI readiness" | "Roll out AI training by [timeframe]" |
| "Capability building at scale" | "Get 2,800 people trained without burning out 6" |
| "Transformative skills strategy" | "The dashboard your CFO actually reads" |
| Industry stat (88%, 91%) | Named-customer before/after |
| Glass tower / cityscape visual | Home office / open-plan mid-size HQ |
| Board deck language | Slack-thread language |

### 7.2 The 5-Second Rule

Every MM ad must pass this test: In the first 5 seconds of reading, Maya thinks "this is for me." That means:

- The headline names her role, her team size, or her problem — not an industry trend
- The visual shows someone who looks like her, in a place that looks like where she works
- The metric is one she personally tracks (completion rate, time-to-launch, hours saved) — not a macro-stat she'd put in a board deck

### 7.3 The "Don't Be Boring" Checklist

Adapted from the Brand Voice Guide for MM:

- Does the headline make Maya stop scrolling? If it could run in any LMS vendor's ad, it's too generic.
- Does the body copy use Maya's language (left column from the tribal language table)?
- Is the proof point a named peer's result — not an industry stat?
- Does the visual show a real human in a real-looking mid-market office — not a glass tower?
- Could David Ogilvy look at this ad and find one unexpected, memorable element? (If not, add one.)
- Would Maya screenshot this and send it to her CHRO? If no, it's not shareable enough.

## 8. Ad Concept Frameworks for MM

Five starter concepts aligned to the MM positioning hierarchy. These are wireframe-level — design owns the final art. All are LinkedIn Single Image (1200×627).

### Concept 1: "Your LMS Is Where Learning Goes to Die"

- **Type:** Pain (Nightmare Scenario principle)
- **Headline:** Your LMS is where learning goes to die.
- **Overlay:** Acme went from 12% to 94%.
- **Body:** 31% average completion. Sunday night emails asking why. Sound familiar? One MM company changed that in 6 months — without adding headcount.
- **Visual direction:** Dimly lit office, single monitor with a stale LMS dashboard, sticky notes everywhere. Real, not stock.

### Concept 2: "6 People. 2,800 Learners. Zero Burnout."

- **Type:** Benefit (Painful DIY principle)
- **Headline:** How [Customer] does L&D lean.
- **Overlay:** 6 people. 2,800 learners. Zero burnout.
- **Body:** Enterprise L&D teams have 30 people. You have 6. Docebo's AI closes the gap so your team ships training — not spreadsheets.
- **Visual direction:** Small, energetic team in a bright open-plan office. Laptop screens showing clean dashboards. No glass towers.

### Concept 3: "The Screenshot That Ended the Argument"

- **Type:** Brand (Visual Proof principle)
- **Headline:** What L&D leaders show their CFO.
- **Overlay:** The screenshot that ended the argument.
- **Body:** When your CFO asks "what did we get for that training spend?" — you need an answer that fits in one Slack message. Docebo builds that answer automatically.
- **Visual direction:** Close-up of a phone screen showing a clean analytics dashboard with clear ROI metrics. Over-the-shoulder shot. Natural lighting.

### Concept 4: "If You Have to Force Attendance, Your Training Sucks"

- **Type:** Pain (Status Quo Roast principle)
- **Headline:** Make it the perk, not the punishment.
- **Overlay:** If you have to force attendance, your training sucks.
- **Body:** Your employees dread mandatory training. What if they asked for more? Docebo customers see completion rates jump from 31% to 90%+ because the experience doesn't feel like homework.
- **Visual direction:** Split image: left side shows bored employees in a dim training room; right side shows engaged person on a laptop in a bright space, smiling.

### Concept 5: "Your AI Strategy Is a Slide. Where's the Rollout?"

- **Type:** Benefit (Reality Check principle)
- **Headline:** Ship learning by Q3, not 2027.
- **Overlay:** Your AI strategy is a slide. Where's the rollout?
- **Body:** Every exec has an AI deck. Very few have an AI rollout their people actually completed. Docebo gets you from strategy to shipped training in weeks, not quarters.
- **Visual direction:** VP-looking professional standing in front of a whiteboard with "Q3" circled. Confident, real, mid-career. Approachable office setting.

## 9. Creative Scoring Criteria for MM Ads

Every MM ad in the Creative Monitor should be scored on these dimensions before approval. All scores are 1–10.

| Dimension | What a 10 Looks Like |
|---|---|
| Brand voice framework usage | Uses MM pillar rewrites, not ENT defaults |
| First-5-second captivation | Maya thinks "this is for me" before she finishes the headline |
| Hormozi score | Clear dream outcome, low perceived effort, urgency |
| Hook strength | Pattern interrupt — stops the scroll with a tribal-language phrase |
| Avatar resonance | Maya would screenshot and send to her CHRO |
| Pain specificity | Names the exact problem (completion rates, steerco, Sunday emails) |
| "This is for me" factor | Role, team size, or timeline called out explicitly |
| Callout clarity | One second to identify as target audience |
| Ogilvy copy rating | Unexpected element; memorable; not interchangeable with competitors |
| Anti-boring rating (Ogilvy) | Would stand out in a feed of blue-gradient B2B ads |

> A score below 6 on any dimension means the ad goes back for revision. A score below 6 on "Anti-boring" is an automatic kill.

## 10. Visual Direction for MM Creative

The current enterprise visual palette (glass towers, robot arms, sunset cities) reads as "this isn't for me" to a mid-market VP. The MM visual palette should feel closer to home.

### 10.1 Do's

- Real humans who look like mid-career VPs/Directors (40s, approachable, not C-suite-polished)
- Open-plan offices, home offices, mid-size company HQs — not Manhattan glass towers
- Laptop and phone screens showing clean dashboards with real-looking metrics
- Natural lighting, warm tones, slight depth of field
- Over-the-shoulder shots, candid angles — not posed stock photography
- Docebo brand colors (Navy #0033A0, Blue #0057FF, warm neutrals) applied with restraint

### 10.2 Don'ts

- No glass towers, skyline shots, or abstract AI imagery
- No stock photos of people in suits around a boardroom table
- No blue gradients that look like every other B2B ad
- No robot hands, circuit boards, or "AI brain" visuals
- No all-text creative — every ad needs a human face or a real screen

## 11. Implementation & Testing Plan

### 11.1 How to Use This Guide

1. Tag all mid-market creative as "MM" in the Docebo Creative Monitor.
2. For any MM-tagged asset, use this guide as the primary voice reference. Defer to the original Brand Voice Guide and Phoenix Brand Guide only where this document is silent.
3. Score every MM ad against the 10 dimensions in Section 9 before creative review.
4. Run the tribal language litmus test (Section 4): if a headline could appear in the right column, rewrite it.

### 11.2 Validation Needed Before Launch

This guide is built on the buyer persona PDFs and brand voice guide. The following items need confirmation from CS, RevOps, or PMM before campaign launch:

- Maya as primary MM signer: CS to confirm VP/Director of L&D is the decision-maker at 1k–5k, vs. Customer Education or CRO personas
- 3 named MM customer logos for social proof (currently using [Customer] placeholders)
- MM competitive frame: hypothesis is Absorb, SkillJar, TalentLMS, LearnUpon dominate MM consideration. Confirm with RevOps win/loss data.
- Per-ad MM engagement data to identify which current ads are closest to working for MM
- CTV exclusion: Manifesto film ran ENT-only. Recommend separate 30s MM-specific re-cut before scaling CTV to MM.

### 11.3 Testing Cadence

- Week 1–2: Launch 5 MM-only ad concepts from Section 8
- Day 14: First read on engagement rate vs. ENT baseline (target: >2% account engagement)
- Day 28: Statistical significance check; kill bottom 2 performers, iterate top 3
- Day 60: Full report comparing MM engagement rate, branded search lift, and pipeline contribution vs. pre-test period
